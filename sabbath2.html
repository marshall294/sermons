Sabbath vs. First Day of the Week

Home

Presenter: Brother Marshall Jacob

This page provides a comparative analysis of the Sabbath and the First Day of the Week, exploring their origins, significance, and practices within the Christian faith. Understanding these differences can provide insight into their theological implications and their roles in contemporary worship.

The Sabbath

The Sabbath is a day of rest observed on the seventh day of the week, from Friday evening to Saturday evening. It is rooted in the Old Testament, where it is established as a day of rest and worship as commanded by God:

Exodus 20:8-10 – "Remember the Sabbath day by keeping it holy. Six days you shall labor and do all your work, but the seventh day is a sabbath to the Lord your God."

In Jewish tradition, the Sabbath was a day for cessation from work and was considered a sign of the covenant between God and Israel (Exodus 31:16-17).

The First Day of the Week

The First Day of the Week, known as Sunday, is significant in Christianity for commemorating the resurrection of Jesus Christ. Early Christians began gathering on this day to celebrate the resurrection, marking a shift from the traditional Sabbath observance:

Acts 20:7 – "On the first day of the week we came together to break bread. Paul spoke to the people, and because he intended to leave the next day, kept on talking until midnight."

This shift from Saturday to Sunday as a day of worship symbolizes the new creation and the new covenant established through Christ's resurrection (Revelation 1:10).

Comparative Analysis

While the Sabbath was observed as a day of rest and worship under the old covenant, the First Day of the Week represents the new covenant brought about by Jesus Christ. The early Church’s shift from observing the Sabbath to celebrating the resurrection on Sunday reflects the theological transition from the old covenant to the new:

  • Old Covenant: The Sabbath was a commandment under the Mosaic Law, emphasizing rest and cessation from work (Deuteronomy 5:12-15).
  • New Covenant: The First Day of the Week symbolizes the fulfillment of the Law through Christ and the beginning of a new era in Christian worship (1 Corinthians 16:2).

Understanding this shift helps to appreciate the early Church's adaptation and the theological reasons behind moving the primary day of worship from Saturday to Sunday.

Conclusion

The Sabbath and the First Day of the Week represent different aspects of religious observance in the Judeo-Christian tradition. The Sabbath reflects the Old Testament laws and covenant, while the First Day of the Week embodies the new covenant and the celebration of Christ’s resurrection. This transition highlights the dynamic nature of faith and worship as guided by biblical principles.
